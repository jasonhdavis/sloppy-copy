Dr. Mara Voss — Planetary Geologist, Lunar Structure Analyst
Bio

Dr. Mara Voss is a planetary geologist specializing in lunar impact basins, crustal density mapping, and orbital geophysics. Early in her career she worked on data interpretation from several lunar missions, analyzing seismic readings, gravitational anomalies, and crustal thickness variations.

Over time, Voss became increasingly unsettled by certain characteristics of the Moon that appear difficult to reconcile with conventional planetary formation models. In particular, she focuses on unusual density distribution patterns, large-scale resonance phenomena observed during lunar impact experiments, and the Moon’s highly stable orbital characteristics.

While most planetary scientists interpret these as natural outcomes of early solar system dynamics, Voss believes the evidence may indicate something more unusual: that the Moon may contain extensive internal structures inconsistent with a purely natural body.

She does not argue that the Moon is entirely artificial. Instead, she proposes that large-scale internal modifications or installations may exist beneath the lunar crust, potentially built by unknown ancient technological actors.

Her work focuses on identifying geological anomalies that might indicate large subsurface voids, engineered cavities, or structural frameworks embedded within the lunar interior.

Lunar Anomaly Background (Used for Context)

Several unusual features of the Moon often appear in Voss’s analysis:

Seismic Ringing

During Apollo missions, impact experiments caused the Moon to vibrate for unusually long periods, sometimes described metaphorically as “ringing like a bell.”

Voss interprets this not literally, but as evidence that the Moon may contain large internal structural cavities or discontinuities.

Mascons

Large impact basins contain mass concentrations that create measurable gravitational anomalies.

Voss suggests these anomalies may indicate dense subsurface structures rather than simple geological compression.

Orbital Stability

The Moon’s orbit and tidal locking are remarkably stable.

Voss occasionally speculates that such stability may indicate intentional orbital positioning.

She frames this as an engineering question rather than a mystical one.

Recurring Narrative Themes

Many articles suggest the Moon may contain ancient infrastructure or observational systems embedded within the crust.

Possible interpretations Voss explores:

large subterranean chambers

structural reinforcement networks

dormant technological systems

ancient monitoring installations

She does not speculate on the builders’ identity with certainty.

Instead, she raises the possibility that the Moon may function as a long-term astronomical observation platform.
